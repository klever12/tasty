Purpose

Our goal was to create an easy and safe way for those who attend the College of Wooster to get meals from Mom's on the go!

Features

Intuitive menu with the option to see only vegetarian options, only vegan option, only gluten free options, or everything Mom's has to offer

Remote paying with mea swipes, flex dollars, or both

Immediate updates of remaining meal plan balance

Estimated time required for the order just placed to me made and ready to be picked up

Design Decisions and Details

The page that appears when the app is opened in simple, clear, and easy to navigate. The logo shows what the app is for - food from Mom's - with a playful nod to the college's unofficial mascot. The green button draws attention and tells the user what to do next. The red text is positioned diretly below the button to make it very difficult to miss. This text lets the user know that some items may be unavailable, without making them go through the trouble of trying to place an order before realizing they cannot have what they originally opened the app to get.

The menu has checkboxes right at the top to allow users to trim the menu down to only what their food restrictions allow. This reduces clutter and allows those with food restrictions to not have to scroll through many dishes that they cannot eat. The big, bold headers give clear information about what type of food the user is looking at and catch the eye when scrolling fast, allowing users to quickly scan for and find what they may be looking for. The menu itslef has its food listings in the same order as the listings are on the official Mom's menu, as well as the one listed on the campus dining website. This provides familiarity and, again, allows users to quickly find what they are looking for. The dropdown that appears when hovering over a dish allows users to see what they may want to try, or may dislike. Clicking on a menu item provides feedback in the form of a "press" animation. A pop-up window that appears on click and allows the user to enter food customization draws the attention of the user, telling them something must be completed before continuing. (more about the customization page here?) Once submitted, the green banner appears on the top of the screen is a form of feedback, telling the user that their dish of choice was successfully added to their order. The green color denotes success of the action, and banner at the top does not get in the way, since it is simply a notification and no action in required. A button on the top and bottom of the menu shows the number of dishes in their oder, and may be clicked to move to the checkout page. Having one at the top and one at the bottom allows the user to continue to the checkout without having to scroll all the way back to where they were before.

The checkout page of the application greets the user with three useful sections: the order information portion, payment buttons, and the order buttons. The order information displays the food items, quantity, and price. In addition, the table gives the user the freedom to remove specific items they no longer need. The payment buttons allow the user to either pay with swipes and/or flex, which is displayed at the top of the screen. If they change their mind on their payment method, they are allowed to restart. Finally, the user can either return to the menu and add more items to their order, or they can finalize their order with the "place order" button. The buttons are seperated in sections, so the buttons with similar functionality are grouped together.

The order-placed page is a simpler version of the checkout page. Instead of buttons that allow for payment methods, this page allows the user to place another order since their previous order has been concluded. The user can inspect what items they have in their finalized order, and an estimated time for the order is also displayed.
